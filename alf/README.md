# The Augmented K–16 AI Learning Framework: Scenario Tools

## Overview

The **Augmented K–16 AI Learning Framework: Scenario Tools** is a set of self-contained HTML pages designed to help education leaders, curriculum specialists, teachers, facilitators, novice educators, and instructional support teams move from AI conversation to practical, printable products.

These pages are intended to support planning, reflection, responsible AI adoption, instructional design, classroom facilitation, and intervention planning across K–16 settings.

This project is a **thought experiment and practical prototype** based on Paul Roetzer’s foundational work around AI literacy, AI transformation, and professional role-based AI adoption. It adapts those business-facing ideas into a K–16 education context, with emphasis on responsible use, instructional purpose, privacy, student learning, and human judgment.

## Purpose

The scenario tools are designed to help role-based users create concrete outputs rather than simply generate scripts or discussion prompts.

Each tool includes canned, selectable problem-solving items that can be used to generate print-ready artifacts such as:

- roadmaps
- risk registers
- implementation checklists
- redesigned assignment briefs
- student process rubrics
- station cards
- differentiation supports
- debate protocols
- meeting briefs
- intervention grouping plans
- teacher review checklists
- exit tickets
- follow-up communications

Optional dialogue or script supports are included where helpful, but the primary goal is to produce usable products.

## The Six Roles

The main framework organizes K–16 AI adoption around six practical roles.

### 1. The Leader

The Leader is the visionary and policy maker who builds safe infrastructure, defines expectations, clarifies tool approval, and establishes conditions for responsible adoption.

Related tool:

- `ai-adoption-roadmap-builder.html`

Printable products:

- adoption phase snapshot
- AI roadmap
- risk register
- stakeholder communication checklist
- pilot selection criteria

### 2. The Architect

The Architect is the instructional designer or curriculum specialist who protects rigor and redesigns learning experiences so AI supports, rather than replaces, thinking.

Related tool:

- `research-essay-redesign-tool.html`

Printable products:

- redesigned assignment brief
- process checkpoint map
- AI-use disclosure
- process rubric
- oral defense prompts

### 3. The Builder

The Builder is the AI-empowered educator who creates classroom supports, source-grounded activities, differentiated materials, and unit helpers.

Related tool:

- `ecosystem-unit-helper-builder.html`

Printable products:

- TEKS/NGSS-aligned unit helper
- station cards
- vocabulary supports
- differentiation menu
- ACE reflection prompts
- exit ticket

### 4. The Orchestrator

The Orchestrator facilitates learning environments where students critique, verify, discuss, and improve AI-generated ideas.

Related tool:

- `third-thinker-debate-tool.html`

Printable products:

- debate protocol
- student role cards
- evidence audit sheet
- discussion norms
- revision task
- exit ticket

### 5. The Apprentice

The Apprentice is the novice educator or pre-service teacher developing professional judgment, relationship skills, and adaptive instructional decision-making.

Related tool:

- `parent-conference-simulator.html`

Printable products:

- parent conference brief
- agenda
- solution menu
- talking points
- follow-up email
- optional practice prompts

### 6. The Agents

The Agents are digital scaffolds that assist with grouping, analysis, differentiation, summarization, and intervention planning while keeping the educator in charge.

Related tool:

- `misconception-grouping-tool.html`

Printable products:

- misconception grouping map
- intervention station cards
- teacher review checklist
- regrouping guide
- exit ticket

## Education Frameworks and Instructional Sources

The tools connect AI use to established instructional practices and learning frameworks.

### SOLO Taxonomy

The SOLO Taxonomy provides a way to describe levels of student understanding, from simple or surface responses to more connected, extended, and transferable understanding.

Source:

- [Pam Hook: SOLO Taxonomy](https://pamhook.com/solo-taxonomy/)

Use in these tools:

- research assignment redesign
- student process rubrics
- reflection prompts
- evaluation of student reasoning
- movement from idea listing to connected explanation

### ACE: Articulate, Connect, Extend

ACE is used here as a simple reflection structure:

- **Articulate:** What did I learn?
- **Connect:** How does this connect to what I already know?
- **Extend:** How can I apply or transfer this learning?

Source:

- [Miguel Guhlin: AI and Learning / ACE reference](https://mguhlin.org/2026/05/13/kpft-radio-guest-ai-and-learning/)

Use in these tools:

- ecosystem unit reflections
- AI-supported learning summaries
- student exit tickets
- analog follow-up activities

### Visible Learning and High-Effect Size Instructional Strategies

The tools reference high-impact instructional strategies such as teacher clarity, feedback, formative assessment, visible thinking, classroom discussion, and metacognitive reflection.

Source:

- [Visible Learning MetaX: Influences](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/Influences)

Use in these tools:

- formative assessment grouping
- feedback protocols
- visible reasoning routines
- debate and critique activities
- teacher review checklists

Source:

- [TCEA: Student Privacy Shield — TCEA PROTECT](https://blog.tcea.org/student-privacy-shield-tcea-protect/)

Use in these tools:

- AI adoption roadmap

- approved-tool planning
- privacy and safety checklist

### SHINE Framework

The SHINE Framework is used as a responsible AI adoption lens for evaluating AI-powered education tools and ensuring that implementation decisions include safety, human judgment, instructional alignment, and stakeholder awareness.

Source:

- [TCEA: SHINE — Lighting the Way to Responsible AI Adoption](https://blog.tcea.org/shine-lighting-the-way-to-responsible-ai-adoption/)

Use in these tools:

- responsible AI adoption planning

4. AI Unification
5. AI Everywhere

Source:

- [TCEA: Responsible AI Adoption](https://blog.tcea.org/responsible-ai-adoption/)

Use in these tools:

- district adoption roadmap

- pilot-to-scale implementation planning

## Tool Design Philosophy

These tools were designed with several principles in mind.

### 1. Product over prompt

The goal is to produce something useful that an educator can print, share, or adapt.

Examples:

- not just “write a parent conference script”
- instead: generate a meeting brief, concern summary, agenda, solution menu, follow-up email, and optional practice prompts

### 2. Canned choices with flexible output

Each tool includes prebuilt issue categories, common problems, and practical response options. This keeps the tools fast and usable during planning, coaching, or professional learning.

### 3. Human judgment remains central

The tools generate drafts and planning products. Educators remain responsible for reviewing, adapting, approving, and implementing all outputs.

### 4. AI as support, not replacement

The tools model AI as a scaffold for professional work, not as a substitute for educator expertise, instructional decision-making, relationship-building, or student support.

### 5. Print-ready by default

Outputs are designed to be copied, printed, saved as PDF, or used as meeting and classroom handouts.

## Local Adaptation Guidance

Before using these tools in a school or district setting:

1. Replace generic standards references with local curriculum standards.
2. Confirm approved AI tools and data-use rules.
3. Remove any tool names that are not locally approved.
4. Review outputs for accessibility.
5. Check outputs against local board policy.
6. Confirm privacy expectations before using student information.
7. Avoid entering personally identifiable student data into unapproved AI systems.
8. Have educators review and revise all generated products before use.

## Privacy and Safety Notes

These pages are designed as self-contained HTML tools. Depending on the final implementation, they may run locally in the browser without sending form entries to a server. However, users should verify the actual hosting environment before entering sensitive information.

Recommended practice:

- Do not enter student names.
- Do not enter student ID numbers.
- Do not enter protected health information.
- Do not enter disciplinary records.
- Use initials, pseudonyms, or generic group labels when planning.
- Use only locally approved AI systems for student-related work.
- Apply the TCEA PROTECT Rubric before adopting new AI tools.

## Accessibility Notes
